Meet the shiner surfperch

Cymatogaster aggregata

Animal Behavior

The most abundant surfperch along the California coast, shiner surfperch live in loose schools in sloughs and bays and around pilings. Shiner surfperch can live in both salty ocean water and the fresher water in coastal wetlands.

Diet

Small crustaceans, arrow worms, amphipods, fish eggs

Appearance

A male shiner surfperch changes color with the seasons. Come summer, he covers his shiny silver and yellow stripes with a darker suit of courtship colors.

Meet the bay pipefish

Syngnathus leptorhynchus

Animal Behavior

A female pipefish courts the male. If the courting is successful, she deposits up to 721 eggs in a brood pouch on the underside of the male’s body. Then a protective tissue forms over the pouch opening and seals the eggs inside. The male incubates the eggs and even supplies nourishment to the embryos via an attachment to his abdominal wall and bloodstream. The eggs hatch in about six weeks depending on water temperature. A pipefish has tiny dorsal and pectoral fins that beat rapidly as it leisurely swims — either vertically or (mostly) horizontally. A pipefish steers by moving its head from side to side.

Diet

Small crustaceans

Appearance

At first glance, you might not notice a bay pipefish gliding through the eelgrass — its long, pencil-slim body and greenish color mimic a swaying blade. In place of scales, jointed, bonelike rings encircle this fish’s body. To eat, a hungry pipefish gets its tubular, toothless mouth an inch or so from its prey — and slurps.
